# Pigment Content Style Guide

*v1 — July 2026. The single source of truth for how Pigment sounds. Every artist card, artwork note, list, Persona, museum page and daily paragraph inherits from this document. When in doubt, return here.*

---

## 1. What Pigment sounds like

Pigment is written by **one intelligent, aesthetic, playful, slightly obsessive person** — someone who has spent too long in museums, loves telling you why, and respects your time.

The one-sentence test for any piece of copy:

> **Would you send this to a friend who half-cares about art — and would it make them care a little more?**

If it reads like a textbook, a tourist brochure, a meme account, or a press release, rewrite it.

### The four register dials

| Dial | Set to | Not |
|---|---|---|
| Intelligent | assumes the reader is smart | assumes the reader is trained |
| Aesthetic | every sentence earns its rhythm | purple prose |
| Playful | one sharp joke that carries information | joke stacking, memes |
| Obsessive | the detail nobody else mentions | completeness for its own sake |

### Canon examples (already live on the site — this is the voice)

- *"Jack the Dripper — the canvas on the floor, the painter inside the painting"* — tagline: nickname, image, idea, eleven words.
- *"The wombat, 'Top', reportedly slept in the dinner-table epergne"* — fact: absurd, specific, hedged with "reportedly."
- *"Six thousand seas, painted entirely from memory"* — number + paradox = instant intrigue.
- *"solitary painters (Hilma af Klint kept her circle a séance) aren't shown"* — UI caption doing emotional work.

## 2. Universal rules

1. **Lead with the image, not the category.** Bad: "X was an important Baroque painter." Good: "X painted saints with dirty feet."
2. **Numbers beat adjectives.** "Some 6,000 canvases" > "extremely prolific." "Died at 27" > "tragically young."
3. **One idea per sentence; vary sentence length.** A short sentence after two long ones lands like a brushstroke.
4. **No academic throat-clearing.** Ban openers: "It is widely considered…", "Throughout history…", "One of the most important…"
5. **No brochure adjectives.** Ban: *stunning, breathtaking, iconic, masterpiece* (unless ironic), *must-see, world-renowned.*
6. **The second person is allowed and encouraged** where the reader is doing something: "Look for…", "You'll notice…", "Click anything; everything connects."
7. **Present tense for artworks, past tense for lives.** The painting *does*; the painter *did*.
8. **The em-dash is our signature punctuation** — one per unit of copy, two at most. It marks the turn.
9. **Diacritics are always correct.** Doré, Kırmızı, Müşfik, Kngwarreye, Ōi. No exceptions, no ASCII laziness.
10. **Every unit of copy should contain at least one thing the reader can *see*.** Abstract praise is invisible; "a neck like a column, eyes like blank almonds" is not.

---

## 3. Accuracy & honesty rules

Pigment is playful but never careless. Trust is the product.

1. **Hedge legends, state facts.** Legends get *reportedly, legend says, Vasari claims, the story goes.* Documented facts get plain statement. Never let a good story quietly upgrade itself into a fact.
2. **Never invent quotes.** If a quote's attribution is shaky, say so ("attributed," "the quote varies; the wheelchair didn't").
3. **Contested identities are stated as contested.** (Malevich: "Both Ukraine and Russia claim him.") Choose a primary filing, acknowledge the dispute, never flatten it.
4. **Copyright is worn openly.** We show public-domain works and say so; generative covers are "interpretations painted in the browser," never passed off as the real thing.
5. **Uncertainty is content.** "No one knows why" is often the most interesting sentence available. Use it.
6. **No fake precision.** "c. 1503–19" not "1503." If scholarship ranges, the date ranges.
7. **When correcting the record, don't lecture.** One clause of correction, then move on.

## 4. Per-content-type specifications

Word budgets are targets, not laws — but a unit 2× over budget is a rewrite, not an exception.

### 4.1 Artist tagline
- **Format:** `[image or claim] — [twist or consequence]`, ≤ 14 words.
- Already canonical across 231 artists. New taglines must not break the register.

### 4.2 "Why they matter" card *(new, Tier 1 artists)*
- **40–60 words. One paragraph.**
- Must answer: what changed because this person painted?
- Structure: the claim → the evidence you can see → the lineage ("without X, no Y").
- ✅ *"Caravaggio detonated European painting in a single decade: street kids as angels, biblical violence staged in cellar darkness under one brutal shaft of light. Every noir shadow — Rembrandt's, Ribera's, cinema's — runs on his electricity."*
- ❌ *"Caravaggio was a revolutionary artist whose innovative use of chiaroscuro had an enormous influence on the history of Western art."* (Says nothing you can see.)

### 4.3 "Look for" — visual traits *(new, Tier 1 artists & movements)*
- **3–5 bullets, each ≤ 8 words, each a thing the eye can find.**
- ✅ *Blank almond eyes · Necks like columns · Backgrounds that refuse detail*
- ❌ *Emotional intensity · Unique style · Masterful technique* (not findable by looking)

### 4.4 Artwork page — short explanation *(new)*
- **50–80 words.** What is happening, why it stopped traffic then, why it still does.
- Follow with **"What to notice": 3 bullets**, same rules as Look-for but work-specific ("the dog nobody in the painting notices," "her sleeve takes more paint than her face").

### 4.5 Editorial lists
- **The title is the product.** It must work as a text message. Mood-first, never taxonomic. ✅ *Paintings that look like they know your secret.* ❌ *Selected Symbolist works.*
- **Description: 40–70 words**, establishing the mood and the rule of admission ("everything here would ruin a dinner party in the best way").
- **Per-artwork blurb: 1–2 sentences**, connecting the work to the list's premise — not a mini-biography.
- A list may be funny OR devastating; the best are both. Every list needs one unexpected inclusion (the non-obvious pick is the curator's signature).

### 4.6 Movement explainers *(upgraded, top movements)*
- **"In one sentence":** ≤ 25 words, no jargon, must contain the movement's *desire* (what it wanted), not just its features.
- **Look for:** 4–5 visual bullets.
- **Start with:** 3–5 artists in recommended order, arrows allowed: *Monet → Morisot → Degas.*
- **Go next:** 2–3 movements with one clause of reason each.
- **"What it was reacting against"** — one short paragraph. Every movement is an argument; name the opponent.

### 4.7 Museum pages *(new)*
- Informative, never brochure. The register: **a well-read friend who's been there twice and has opinions.**
- The hero unit is **"If you only have one hour"** — 5–8 works, ordered as a route, one sentence each, honest about crowds ("you will not be alone with her").
- History: 60–100 words. Architecture: one paragraph only if the building matters. Fun facts: same rules as artist facts.

### 4.8 Persona descriptions *(Phase 1.5)*
- **35–55 words, second person.**
- Rules: one concrete anchor (artist, artwork, or habit) + one flattering truth + **one gentle flaw or tease.** No horoscope vagueness — if it could describe anyone, it describes no one.
- ✅ *"You trust a shadow more than a smile. Your favorite paintings have a body count, a single candle, and better lighting than any film you've seen this year. You say you'd survive the 17th century. You would not."*
- ❌ *"You are a deep and passionate soul who loves beauty in all its forms."* (Horoscope. Delete.)

### 4.9 Painting of the Day paragraph *(new)*
- **60–90 words.** Must land **one detail you can't unsee** — after reading, the viewer's eye should go somewhere specific.
- End with motion when possible: a question, a dare, a place to click.
- Seasonal awareness allowed and encouraged (October gets October paintings).

### 4.10 UI microcopy
- Buttons are verbs, warm but short: *Admire · Seen in person · Take the test · Go deeper.*
- Empty states get personality, one line: *"Nothing admired yet — your eye is still a blank canvas."*
- Captions may be poetic; errors may not. Errors are plain and helpful.

## 5. Humor calibration

- **A joke must carry information.** "Boterismo — insists it's volume, not fat" teaches while it smiles.
- **One joke per unit of copy.** Two if the unit is a list description. Never three.
- Punch up or sideways (institutions, the art market, critics, ourselves) — never at artists' tragedies, identities, or at the reader's ignorance.
- Death, war, genocide, mental illness: no jokes, no dramatic flourishes either. Plain sentences carry these best (see Rublev, Gorky, Kollwitz pages — that's the register).
- Memes date; rhythm doesn't. If a joke depends on 2026 internet, cut it.

## 6. Turkish flavor

- Subtle, ambient, never performative: an Istanbul-at-6-PM list, Türkçe names always spelled right, the Şakir Paşa family treated as the dynasty it is.
- Turkish-context pages may carry slightly more local warmth; nothing should require Turkish to be understood.
- A future TR locale is an architectural possibility — never hard-weld English strings into logic.

## 7. Terminology & conventions

| Convention | Rule |
|---|---|
| "painter" vs "artist" | *painter* when true; *artist* for multi-medium figures |
| movement names | capitalized: Impressionism, Ottoman Miniature |
| dates | `1832–1883`, `c. 1503–19`, `fl. 1560–1595`; en-dash, `c.` with space |
| living artists | `b. 1937`; timeline bars fade, never close |
| nationality disputes | primary filing + acknowledgment, per §3.3 |
| "seen in person" | always lowercase verb-phrase in UI |
| numbers | figures for years/counts; words for one–nine in prose |
| museum names | local name where famous (Musée d'Orsay, Rijksmuseum) |

## 8. The pre-publish checklist

1. **True?** Every claim either documented or hedged.
2. **Visible?** At least one thing the eye can find.
3. **Human?** Reads aloud without embarrassment.
4. **Tight?** Within budget; every adjective interrogated.
5. **On-voice?** Passes the send-to-a-friend test.
6. **Linked?** Points somewhere next — no dead-end pages.
