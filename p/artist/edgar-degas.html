Edgar Degas

1834–1917 · Dancers from the wings — the Impressionist who hated the outdoors

Degas exhibited with the Impressionists eight times and disliked almost everything about them — plein air, spontaneity, the very name. What he shared was modern life; what he kept was draughtsmanship. Ballet rehearsals, laundresses, café solitude: nobody framed the candid instant with more calculation, or made pastel carry more weight.

Degas called himself a realist and scoffed at painting outdoors, yet co-organised the Impressionist exhibitions and out-modernised them all: ballet rehearsals seen from odd balconies, laundresses yawning, compositions cropped like snapshots under Japanese-print influence.
