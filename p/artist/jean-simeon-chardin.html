Jean-Siméon Chardin

1699–1779 · Copper pots and soap bubbles, painted like sacraments

A Paris carpenter's son who almost never left the city, Chardin rose through the Academy on the strength of a gutted ray-fish painted with horrifying magnificence.

In the century of Boucher's nymphs, Chardin painted kitchen maids, water glasses and children's games with a slow, granular touch that made critics reach for words like 'magic.' Diderot wrote that one stops before a Chardin 'as a weary traveller sits down.' His quiet authority — matter itself made luminous — later anchored Cézanne, Morandi and Lucian Freud.
