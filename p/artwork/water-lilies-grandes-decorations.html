Water Lilies (Grandes Décorations)

Claude Monet · c. 1915–26 · Musée de l'Orangerie, Paris · 200 × 600 cm (panel)

Monet's answer to the First World War: the day after the armistice he confirmed the gift of his vast water-lily cycle to France, built for two oval rooms where the horizon never appears. No sky except as reflection, no bank, no edge — just the pond, wrapped around you at the scale of a war memorial. He painted it half-blind through cataracts, reworking until his death; the rooms opened months after it, and have been called the Sistine Chapel of Impressionism.

  • There is no horizon line anywhere — you float
  • Willows fall as vertical strokes through horizontal water
  • Stand close: chaos. Step back: the pond assembles
