Melencolia I

Albrecht Dürer · 1514 · The Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York · 24 × 18.5 cm (engraving)

The most argued-over engraving ever cut: a winged genius sits slumped among the instruments of geometry and craft — compass idle, polyhedron mute, magic square ticking on the wall — too full of thought to act. Dürer made thinking's dead end into a picture, and four centuries of scholars have proven the point by disagreeing about every object in it. Printed by the hundreds, it made melancholy the artist's official temperament, and the studio's patron mood.

  • The magic square's every row sums to 34 — and its bottom row dates the print: 1514
  • The polyhedron has never been conclusively explained; careers have tried
  • Comet and rainbow at the horizon: hope, or its postponement
