Vision After the Sermon

Paul Gauguin · 1888 · Scottish National Gallery, Edinburgh · 72.2 × 91 cm

Breton women in starched white coifs close their eyes after church — and share one hallucination: Jacob wrestling the angel on a field of pure vermilion. Gauguin painted the ground red because feeling demanded it, fenced the vision off with a diagonal tree trunk borrowed from Japanese prints, and declared painting freed from appearances. He offered the canvas to two village churches; both priests politely refused. Synthetism — color as emotion, outline as scripture — starts here.

  • The ground is red because the sermon was, not the grass
  • The tree trunk divides real women from shared vision
  • The wrestlers come from Hokusai's sketchbooks, via Brittany
