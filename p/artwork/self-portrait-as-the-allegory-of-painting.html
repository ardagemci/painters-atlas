Self-Portrait as the Allegory of Painting

Artemisia Gentileschi · 1638–39 · Royal Collection, Windsor Castle, Windsor · 98.6 × 75.2 cm

Every painter of the era wanted to paint Painting — the allegorical woman with the mask pendant, unruly locks and iridescent sleeve prescribed by the emblem books. Only one artist could claim to BE her. At the English court, Artemisia painted herself as the allegory: leaning hard into the canvas, arm cocked like a fencer's, no simpering, no glance at the viewer. A self-portrait, a manifesto and a checkmate no male rival could answer, all on one canvas.

  • The mask pendant at her throat — 'imitation,' per the emblem books
  • One arm holds the brush high, the other the palette: a human compass
  • She never looks at you; the work gets her whole face
