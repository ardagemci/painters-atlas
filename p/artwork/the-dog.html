The Dog

Francisco Goya · c. 1820–23 · Museo del Prado, Madrid · 131.5 × 79.3 cm

A dog's head, a tilted void, nothing else — painted straight onto the wall of Goya's farmhouse with no title, no buyer and no audience in mind. Nobody knows if the dog is drowning, buried or simply looking up at something we aren't shown; the painting refuses to say. A century before abstraction it is mostly empty field, which is why painters from Miró to the color-field generation have treated it like a relic. It may be the loneliest picture ever made.

  • Find the head; now try to find anything else
  • The 'ground' never explains itself — slope, wave, or grave
  • The dog looks up and to the right, at nothing we can see
