The Birth of Venus

Sandro Botticelli · c. 1485 · Gallerie degli Uffizi, Florence · 172.5 × 278.9 cm

The first monumental female nude of the post-classical West arrives standing on a shell, blown ashore by entwined wind gods — a pagan goddess painted at altarpiece scale, for a Medici villa, in a city run by monks. Botticelli ignored his era's perspective obsession: no depth, no weight, just line — the neck too long, the shoulder impossible, and nobody has ever wanted them corrected. Venus's face reportedly borrows from Simonetta Vespucci, Florence's adored beauty, dead years before at twenty-two.

  • The neck and shoulder are anatomically impossible — and better for it
  • Every windblown rose is outlined and weightless, like enamel
  • Her pose quotes an ancient Venus statue the Medici owned
