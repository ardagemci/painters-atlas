**Part 1 — the eight blocker claims (U1–U8)**

1. **[major] U1 "mobile navigation conceals most destinations behind an unannounced horizontal gesture" — mechanism confirmed, magnitude unmeasured.** `css/styles.css:837` sets `.main-nav{order:3;width:100%;overflow-x:auto;padding-bottom:2px}` at ≤820px and `:838` sets `.main-nav a{…white-space:nowrap}`; `index.html:37-44` holds eight nowrap destinations. Decisively: this codebase *has* a scroll-affordance idiom and did not apply it here — `.strip` gets `mask-image:linear-gradient(…)` at `css:627-628`, `.main-nav` gets nothing. So "unannounced" is supported by the codebase's own internal inconsistency. "Conceals **most**" (≥5 of 8) is arithmetic I cannot do without a browser. — **adapt**: restate as "the mobile nav is a horizontally scrollable strip carrying no affordance, unlike the site's other horizontal container," which is provable today and blocker-eligible. Strike "most destinations" pending Vermeer.

2. **[critical] U2 root overflow — confirmed by construction, and Duchamp's rebuttal is wrong on both of its points.** `css:170` `#app{…padding:34px 28px…}`; `css:840` drops it to `#app{padding:22px 16px…}` at ≤820px. `css:626` `.strip{overflow:hidden;margin:0 -28px 12px;…}` — a negative margin tuned to cancel the *desktop* 28px gutter, and it is never overridden in any media query. At every width ≤820px the strip extends 28px into a 16px gutter: **12px of real overflow per side at 320, 360, 390, 400 and 768**. Duchamp cited "`css/styles.css:73`, which sets `overflow-x:hidden` **at the root**" as evidence the measurement "does not reproduce." Line 73 is inside the `body{…}` block (`css:65-74`), not `html` — and more importantly `overflow-x:hidden` does not *prevent* overflow, it propagates to the viewport and **hides** it, while `document.documentElement.scrollWidth > clientWidth` still reports it. The declaration is symptom-masking, and its presence is weak evidence that overflow was a known past problem. — **accept** as blocker-eligible; **adapt** criterion 19 (see finding 17).

3. **[major] U3 unsourced history — confirmed, and stronger than THEORY_001 states.** `js/artists-1.js:4-16` record shape is `id, name, years, born, died, nation, eras, movements, techniques, style, palette, tagline, works, life, career, outside, facts` — no source field exists. The `facts` array carries hard claims with zero citation ("$450.3 million", "over 7,000 surviving pages", "some thirty dissections"). `js/influences.js:1-8` asserts "Every relationship is grounded in the artist bios elsewhere in the atlas" while the bios carry no citations — circular, as Duchamp correctly found. — **adapt**: THEORY_001 frames this as "a sampled artist profile lacked traceable sources." It is not a sampling result; it is **0% of 246 records by schema**, settled by one grep. This has a direct consequence for Workstream I (finding 12).

4. **[major] U4 generic image attribution — confirmed.** `js/catalog-1.js:14-15`: `image:{ src:"…commons/thumb/…", page:"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Calling_of_Saint_Matthew", status:"pd" }`. `page` points at an English Wikipedia *article*, not the Commons file page, and there is no license, author, or death-year field. The legal reasoning is sound and must not be argued away. One correction to the framing both THEORY_001 and Duchamp use: the schema already carries a per-field provenance marker — `coordsSource:"override"` at `catalog-1.js:17` — so adding `image.license`/`image.source` is *idiomatic to this schema*, not an alien capability. — **accept** the claim; **adapt** the "new capability" framing built on it.

5. **[critical] U5 "search may rank incidental substrings too highly" — refuted as written, and it is masking three worse defects.** `js/app.js:2175-2181` builds two buckets and concatenates `[...starts, ...contains]`: **prefix matches already always outrank substring matches.** Criterion 24's stated concern is satisfied today. What is actually broken: (a) *exactness is not modeled at all* — an exact full-name match is indistinguishable from any other prefix match; (b) within each bucket, order is `INDEX` array order (`:2160-2168` — Artists, Artworks, Lists, Museums, Movements, Techniques, Eras, Nations), so ranking is **by entity type, not relevance**; (c) `.slice(0, 9)` at `:2181` is applied *after* concatenation, so 246 artists can fill all nine slots and **hide every matching artwork, museum and movement** — this puts criterion 25, not 24, at real risk; (d) group headers are emitted only on type *change* (`:2188`), so the same type renders as two separate headers once the bucket boundary is crossed; (e) only `name` is searched, `meta` is ignored, so no artwork is findable by its artist. — **reject** U5's wording; **accept** a rewritten claim naming (b) and (c). This is the clearest evidence in the document that §3.3 was written from memory of the build rather than from the build.

6. **[critical] U6 route-change orientation — half confirmed, half false, and it misses the serious defect.** Confirmed absent across `js/app.js`, `index.html`, `css/styles.css`: `focus()` **0 occurrences**, `tabindex` **0**, `aria-current` **0**, skip-link **0**. `route()` (`js/app.js:1986-2030`) does `app.innerHTML = html` then `window.scrollTo(0,0)` and never moves focus. **But orientation is not absent**: there are **22 `document.title` assignments** covering every route including 404 (`:1976`), which THEORY_001 and Duchamp both missed. The real defect is the one neither named: `index.html:53` declares `<main id="app" class="view-enter" aria-live="polite">`, and `js/app.js:2020` replaces its entire `innerHTML` on every route change — so a screen reader re-announces **the whole page**, which is the precise opposite of criterion 14's "identifies the new page **once**." It compounds: every onboarding tap calls `route()` (`:2959-2971`), so the full page is re-announced **sixteen times** during the deck. — **adapt**: split U6 into "focus is never moved" (confirmed), "page identity is announced but verbosely and repeatedly, via `aria-live` on the app root" (confirmed, worse than claimed), and "orientation is absent" (**false** — titles are comprehensive).

7. **[major] U7 non-color state — confirmed for three surfaces, false for the one that matters most.** Confirmed color-only: `.tone.on` (`css:923` — border-color + background + color only; markup at `js/app.js:2571-2574` is `<button class="tone"><i></i><span>name</span></button>` with no text change and no glyph) — a WCAG 1.4.1 candidate sitting on the onboarding critical path. Also `.tl2-leg.on` (`css:737`, color only) and `.tl2-bar.dim` (`css:762`, opacity only); `.f-btn.on` (`css:694`) adds `font-weight:500`, a non-color cue but a marginal one. Confirmed globally: **zero** `aria-pressed`/`aria-selected`/`aria-expanded` in the entire codebase. **Refuted** for "saved actions": Admire / Seen in person / Save for later change their *text label* with state — `PP_LABELS` at `js/app.js:84-88` (`["Admire","Admired ✓"]`), applied at `:92`, `:1416`, and updated in place at `:2084`. The accessible name changes, so state *is* conveyed non-visually for the three core personal verbs. — **adapt**: keep the claim for tones, filters and legends; strike "saved actions." Separately, nav `.active` (`css:131-132`) uses color plus an underline that is *identical to `:hover`* (`css:130`), so active and hover are visually indistinguishable — a defect neither report names.

8. **[critical] U8 competing nested actions — confirmed as a hard spec violation at a named line, not a judgment call.** `js/app.js:1447-1453`:
   ```
   <a class="entry-card" href="#/artists" style="--ec:var(--gold)">
     … <button class="ec-surprise" data-random-artist>or surprise me →</button> …
   </a>
   ```
   A `<button>` inside an `<a>`. The HTML content model forbids interactive content as a descendant of `<a>`; behaviour is undefined and the accessible name of the anchor predicts a different outcome than the button delivers — exactly criterion 17's wording. — **accept** as a release blocker. It is also among the cheapest fixes in the document: move the button outside the anchor.

**Part 2 — the scope framing (Duchamp's CRITICAL 3 and 4)**

**Part 5 — what THEORY_001 does not say (the highest-value findings)**
